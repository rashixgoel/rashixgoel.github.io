Case Study: Birth Asphyxia with Hypoxic Ischemic Encephalopathy

📊 Complexity: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ Expert 🏥 Setting: NICU 📅 LOS: 8 days

📋 Case Overview

Patient: Male newborn
Gestational Age: 40 weeks 1 day
Birth Weight: 3,520 grams
Delivery: Emergency cesarean

🎯 Key Learning Objectives:

  • Asphyxia coding: P20.- (fetal) vs P21.9 (newborn) based on cord blood values
  • HIE (P91.6) as most responsible diagnosis when present
  • Sequencing multiple serious conditions (HIE, respiratory failure, PPHN)
  • Mechanical ventilation vs CPAP intervention coding
  • When seizures are coded separately vs part of HIE diagnosis

📄 Clinical Documentation

⚠️ Critical Case: This is a severe birth asphyxia case with multi-organ involvement. Pay close attention to diagnosis sequencing and intervention coding!

NEWBORN DISCHARGE SUMMARY

INFANT: Male

MOTHER: 35 years old, G1P0→1

DELIVERY ROOM EVENTS:

APGAR SCORES: 0 at 1 minute, 2 at 5 minutes, 5 at 10 minutes

Resuscitation performed:

  • Positive pressure ventilation initiated immediately
  • NO response after 30 seconds
  • Intubated at 90 seconds of life
  • Chest compressions started at 2 minutes
  • Epinephrine given via ETT at 4 minutes
  • Heart rate improved to >100 bpm by 6 minutes
  • Spontaneous respirations weak, remained intubated

CORD BLOOD GASES (umbilical artery):

  • pH: 6.82 (SEVERE acidosis, normal >7.20)
  • Base deficit: -18 mmol/L (SEVERE, normal <12)
  • pCO2: 88 mmHg (elevated)
  • pO2: 12 mmHg (very low)

DIAGNOSIS in delivery room: SEVERE BIRTH ASPHYXIA

NICU ADMISSION (Day of Life 1):

Initial Assessment:

  • Encephalopathic: Poor tone, no spontaneous movement
  • Pupils sluggish
  • Mechanical ventilation via endotracheal tube
  • Blood pressure unstable, requiring dopamine

Hour 4 of life: Infant developed SEIZURES (tonic, 30 seconds duration). EEG showing abnormal background. Started on phenobarbital.

Hour 6 of life: Started therapeutic hypothermia (whole body cooling to 33.5°C for 72 hours) for neuroprotection.

Additional complications identified:

  • Persistent Pulmonary Hypertension (PPHN): Oxygen saturations 75-80% despite 100% oxygen. Echo confirmed PPHN with right-to-left shunting.
  • Acute Kidney Injury: Urine output <0.5 mL/kg/hr, creatinine rising (124 μmol/L)

Treatment: Inhaled nitric oxide started for PPHN.

DAYS 2-3 (HYPOTHERMIA PHASE):

  • Continued mechanical ventilation
  • Seizures controlled with phenobarbital
  • Therapeutic hypothermia maintained
  • PPHN improving with nitric oxide
  • Urine output improved, creatinine stabilizing

DAY 4 (REWARMING):

Completed 72 hours of hypothermia. Gradual rewarming over 12 hours to normal body temperature.

DAYS 5-6:

  • Neurological status: Improved tone, some spontaneous movement returning
  • Extubated to CPAP on Day 5
  • Weaned off CPAP to room air Day 6
  • Nitric oxide weaned off successfully
  • No further seizures

DAYS 7-8:

  • MRI brain: Moderate changes consistent with HIE (abnormal signal in basal ganglia)
  • Starting to feed orally
  • Follow-up arranged with neurodevelopmental clinic

DISCHARGE DIAGNOSES:

  1. Term male infant, emergency cesarean delivery
  2. Hypoxic Ischemic Encephalopathy (HIE), moderate
  3. Birth asphyxia with severe cord blood acidosis
  4. Neonatal seizures secondary to HIE
  5. Respiratory failure requiring mechanical ventilation
  6. Persistent pulmonary hypertension of the newborn (PPHN)
  7. Acute kidney injury (resolved)

INTERVENTIONS:

  • Therapeutic hypothermia (72 hours)
  • Mechanical ventilation (5 days)
  • Inhaled nitric oxide therapy
  • Anti-seizure medication (phenobarbital)
  • MRI brain
  • Continuous EEG monitoring

DISCHARGE WEIGHT: 3,420 grams

DISCHARGE CONDITION: Improved but requiring close neurodevelopmental follow-up

DISPOSITION: Home with parents, neurology clinic follow-up in 2 weeks

🧠 Coding Rationale & Decision Points

💡 This Case Tests: Understanding HIE vs asphyxia hierarchy, sequencing multiple critical conditions, and when NOT to code certain manifestations!

The Hierarchy: Asphyxia → HIE

What actually happened:

  1. Baby experienced asphyxia (P20.1) during labour
  2. Asphyxia CAUSED brain injury (HIE = P91.6)
  3. HIE became the condition requiring most intensive treatment

From Fletcher & Lokun, Page 30.7-30.8:

"The diagnosis P91.6 Hypoxic ischaemic encephalopathy includes the neurological manifestations of asphyxia."

"It is classified to P91.6 Hypoxic ischaemic encephalopathy of newborn. When this diagnosis is stated, the individual neurological effects are not coded (e.g. seizures)."

Sequencing Rule:

When both asphyxia AND HIE are present:

  • P91.6 (HIE) = Type M (most responsible diagnosis)
  • P20.1 (Asphyxia) = Type 1 (significant comorbidity)

Why This Makes Sense:

  • HIE required therapeutic hypothermia (72 hours)
  • HIE required continuous EEG monitoring
  • HIE required anti-seizure medication
  • HIE determined the 8-day length of stay
  • HIE requires long-term neurodevelopmental follow-up

→ HIE is the condition that drove resource use and LOS, making it the MRDx.

Asphyxia Coding Based on WHEN and CORD BLOOD pH:

Code When to Use Criteria

P21.9
Newborn asphyxia		 After birth (not umbilical cord) Capillary/arterial (not cord) pH <7.0 and/or

This Case:

✅ P20.1 assigned because:

  • Fetal bradycardia noted DURING labour
  • Cord blood (umbilical artery) pH = 6.82 (meets ≤7.0 criteria)
  • Cord blood base deficit = -18 mmol/L (meets ≥12 criteria)
  • Asphyxia occurred before/during delivery, not after birth

From Fletcher & Lokun, Page 30.7:

Assign P20.- Fetal asphyxia when:

  • Umbilical cord arterial pH is ≤7.0 and/or
  • Umbilical cord arterial base deficit is ≥12 mmol/L

Assign P21.9 Newborn asphyxia when:

  • Capillary or arterial (not umbilical cord) pH is <7.0 and/or
  • Capillary or arterial base deficit is >12 mmol/L

Seizures Are Part of HIE - NOT Coded Separately

❌ DO NOT CODE:

  • P90 (Convulsions of newborn)
  • R56.0 (Febrile convulsions)
  • Any other seizure code

Reason: When HIE (P91.6) is diagnosed, seizures are considered a manifestation of the HIE, not a separate condition.

Fletcher & Lokun Explicit Guidance (Page 30.8):

"When this diagnosis [P91.6 HIE] is stated, the individual neurological effects are not coded (e.g. seizures)."

What IS Captured:

  • ✅ P91.6 (HIE) - includes the seizures
  • ✅ Intervention code for EEG monitoring (1.HZ.30.JN)
  • ✅ Phenobarbital would be documented in medication list
✅ Correct Approach: The seizures are documented in the clinical notes and intervention codes, but P91.6 alone captures the neurological manifestations.

Multiple Critical Conditions - How to Order?

This baby had FOUR Type 1 diagnoses:

  • P28.5 (Respiratory failure)
  • P29.30 (PPHN)
  • P96.0 (Acute kidney injury)
  • P20.1 (Fetal asphyxia)

✅ CIHI Standard for Type 1 Diagnoses:

As stated in Fletcher & Lokun page 30.3: "Diagnosis type 1. Other conditions which do not impact the length of stay or treatment are assigned diagnosis type 0."

All Type 1 diagnoses are of EQUAL importance. They can be listed in any order. The sequence doesn't matter because they're all comorbidities that impacted care equally.

Common Sequencing Approaches:

  • By organ system: Respiratory → Cardiovascular → Renal → Other
  • By temporal order: What developed first → What came later
  • By treatment intensity: Most intensive interventions first

→ Any of these is acceptable! The important part is that they're ALL coded as Type 1.

💬 Test Your Understanding

💡 These Questions: Test your ability to handle complex asphyxia and HIE scenarios!

Question 1: Asphyxia WITHOUT HIE

Same scenario but baby does NOT develop HIE - just had low Apgar scores and required brief resuscitation, then recovered quickly. Cord pH 6.85. Discharged Day 2. What's the MRDx?

✅ Answer:

Z38.010 (Single liveborn, cesarean) = Type M (Main)

P20.1 (Fetal asphyxia) = Type 2 (Significant diagnosis)

Rationale: Without HIE or other major complications, the baby is classified as "unhealthy" but Z38.010 remains the MRDx. The asphyxia is significant enough to warrant Type 2 (not Type 1) because it required resuscitation but resolved quickly and didn't drive extended LOS.

Question 2: Capillary Blood Gas Instead

Baby has normal cord gases (pH 7.25). But 2 hours after birth, develops severe respiratory distress. Capillary blood gas shows pH 6.95, base deficit 15. Would you code P20.1 or P21.9?

✅ Answer:

P21.9 (Newborn asphyxia)

NOT P20.1 (Fetal asphyxia)

Rationale: The acidosis occurred AFTER birth (capillary blood gas 2 hours post-delivery), not during labour/delivery. P20.- is reserved for intrauterine/intrapartum asphyxia documented by CORD blood gases. P21.9 is for postnatal asphyxia documented by capillary or arterial (non-cord) blood gases.

Question 3: Borderline Cord pH

Cord pH is 7.05 (just above the 7.0 cutoff), base deficit 10 (below 12 cutoff). Baby had fetal bradycardia and needed resuscitation. Do you code P20.1?

✅ Answer:

NO - Do NOT code P20.1

Rationale: Per Fletcher & Lokun criteria, P20.1 requires EITHER pH ≤7.0 OR base deficit ≥12. This baby has pH 7.05 (doesn't meet ≤7.0) AND base deficit 10 (doesn't meet ≥12). Neither criterion is met, so P20.1 is not assigned even though there was fetal distress. The resuscitation would be coded as interventions (e.g., 1.GZ.31.CB-EP) but the asphyxia diagnosis is not warranted.

What WOULD be coded: P03.89 (Newborn affected by other specified complications of labour and delivery) as Type 0 if documented, or possibly no diagnosis code at all if baby recovered quickly.

Question 4: If Seizures Started BEFORE HIE Diagnosed

In this case, seizures were noted at Hour 4. HIE wasn't officially diagnosed until Hour 6 when hypothermia started. Should you code the seizures separately since they were "first"?

✅ Answer:

NO - Still do NOT code seizures separately

Rationale: Coding is based on the FINAL diagnosis at discharge, not the temporal order of symptom recognition. By discharge, the diagnosis is P91.6 (HIE), and the seizures are understood to be a manifestation of HIE. Per Fletcher & Lokun: "When this diagnosis is stated, the individual neurological effects are not coded (e.g. seizures)." The timing of when clinicians recognized vs named the condition doesn't change the coding.
