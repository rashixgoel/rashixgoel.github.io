Case Study: Laparoscopic Cholecystectomy for Chronic Cholecystitis

📊 Complexity: ⭐⭐⭐⭐ Advanced 🏥 Setting: Surgical Day Care 📅 Duration: Same-day surgery

📋 Case Overview

Patient: 52-year-old female
Procedure: Laparoscopic cholecystectomy
Setting: Surgical Day Care (SDC)
Admission Type: Elective

🎯 Key Learning Objectives:

  • Chronic vs acute cholecystitis code selection (K80.1 vs K80.0)
  • Cholelithiasis vs choledocholithiasis distinction
  • Laparoscopic approach coding (1.OD.89.DA)
  • Intraoperative cholangiogram coding (3.OE.10.WX)
  • When to code operative cholangiogram vs other imaging
  • Understanding "with or without obstruction" in K80 codes

📄 Clinical Documentation

💡 Interactive Challenge: Read through this operative report, then try coding it yourself before viewing the abstract!

OPERATIVE REPORT

PATIENT: 52-year-old female

DATE OF PROCEDURE: April 10, 2026

SURGEON: Dr. Sarah Chen, General Surgery

ANESTHESIA: General anesthesia

PREOPERATIVE DIAGNOSIS:

Chronic cholecystitis with cholelithiasis

POSTOPERATIVE DIAGNOSIS:

Chronic cholecystitis with cholelithiasis (confirmed)

PROCEDURE PERFORMED:

Laparoscopic cholecystectomy with intraoperative cholangiogram

CLINICAL HISTORY:

This 52-year-old female presented to my office 6 weeks ago with a 1-year history of recurrent biliary colic. Patient describes episodic right upper quadrant pain occurring 2-3 times per month, typically after fatty meals, lasting 2-4 hours. Pain is described as constant, dull ache that radiates to the right shoulder blade. Associated with nausea but no vomiting. No fever, jaundice, or changes in stool/urine color.

INVESTIGATIONS:

  • Abdominal ultrasound (March 2026): Multiple gallstones measuring 8mm, 12mm, and 15mm in gallbladder. Thickened gallbladder wall (5mm) consistent with chronic cholecystitis. No pericholecystic fluid. Common bile duct measures 4mm (normal). No intrahepatic ductal dilation.
  • Liver function tests: Normal (ALT 28 U/L, AST 32 U/L, ALP 78 U/L, Bilirubin 12 μmol/L)

PAST MEDICAL HISTORY: Type 2 diabetes mellitus (on metformin), hypertension (controlled), no previous abdominal surgeries

OPERATIVE FINDINGS:

Patient brought to OR, placed supine on table. General anesthesia induced without complication. Abdomen prepped and draped in sterile fashion.

Pneumoperitoneum established via Veress needle at umbilicus. Four trocars inserted: 10mm umbilical port (camera), 10mm epigastric port, 5mm right subcostal port, 5mm right lateral port.

Laparoscopy revealed: Gallbladder distended with multiple stones visualized through the wall. Mild adhesions between gallbladder and omentum (easily separated). Thickened, fibrotic gallbladder wall consistent with chronic inflammation. No signs of acute inflammation or perforation. Liver appeared normal. No free fluid in abdomen.

Dissection: Calot's triangle carefully dissected. Cystic artery identified and clipped x2, then divided. Cystic duct identified and cleared circumferentially.

Intraoperative Cholangiogram: Small incision made in cystic duct. Cholangiogram catheter inserted. Contrast injected under fluoroscopy. FINDINGS: Common bile duct measures 5mm (normal). Good flow of contrast into duodenum. No filling defects. No stones visualized in biliary tree. Intrahepatic ducts normal. Cholangiogram catheter removed.

Cystic duct clipped x2 proximally, then divided between clips. Gallbladder dissected from liver bed using electrocautery. Hemostasis achieved. Liver bed inspected—no bile leak, no active bleeding.

Gallbladder placed in specimen bag and removed through umbilical port. Ports removed under direct visualization. Fascial closure of 10mm port sites with 0-Vicryl. Skin closed with 4-0 Monocryl subcuticular suture.

SPECIMEN:

Gallbladder with multiple stones sent to pathology

ESTIMATED BLOOD LOSS:

Minimal (<50 mL)

COMPLICATIONS:

None

PATIENT DISPOSITION:

Patient transferred to PACU in stable condition. Tolerated procedure well. Discharged home same day after meeting discharge criteria (tolerating oral fluids, pain controlled with oral analgesia, ambulating independently, voiding spontaneously).

PATHOLOGY REPORT (Final):

Gross: Gallbladder measuring 9 x 4 x 3 cm. Wall is thickened (up to 5mm). Mucosa shows granular, yellow-brown appearance. Multiple gallstones ranging from 0.8 to 1.5 cm in size.

Microscopic: Sections show chronic cholecystitis with marked fibrosis of the gallbladder wall, chronic inflammatory infiltrate, and Rokitansky-Aschoff sinus formation. No acute inflammation. No dysplasia or malignancy identified.

Diagnosis: Chronic cholecystitis with cholelithiasis

DISCHARGE DIAGNOSIS:

1. Chronic cholecystitis with cholelithiasis

2. Type 2 diabetes mellitus (stable, continued home medications)

📊 Coding Abstract

💡 Key Decision: This is CHRONIC cholecystitis (not acute), confirmed by pathology. Code selection depends on stone location and presence of obstruction!

🧠 Coding Rationale & Decision Points

💡 Critical Skill: Differentiating between acute vs chronic cholecystitis and understanding when obstruction is present!

The Critical Distinction: Acute vs Chronic

From ICD-10-CA Index:

K80.0 = Calculus of gallbladder with ACUTE cholecystitis
K80.1 = Calculus of gallbladder with OTHER cholecystitis (chronic)

Evidence This is CHRONIC (not acute):

Clinical Feature This Case Interpretation
History 1-year history of recurrent biliary colic (2-3x/month) Chronic - ongoing, recurrent symptoms
Ultrasound Thickened gallbladder wall (5mm), no pericholecystic fluid Chronic - fibrosis, no acute inflammation
Operative findings Thickened, fibrotic wall. NO acute inflammation or perforation Chronic - no signs of acute process
Pathology "Chronic cholecystitis with marked fibrosis. NO acute inflammation." Chronic - confirmed by pathologist
✅ The Rule: Pathology report is the GOLD STANDARD. If pathology says "chronic cholecystitis," code as chronic (K80.1x), even if clinical presentation seemed acute.
❌ Common Error: Coding all cholecystectomies as "acute" (K80.0x)
✅ Correct: Check pathology report! Many elective cholecystectomies are for chronic cholecystitis.

Understanding "With or Without Obstruction"

K80.10 = Calculus of gallbladder with chronic cholecystitis, WITHOUT mention of obstruction
K80.11 = Calculus of gallbladder with chronic cholecystitis, WITH obstruction

What Counts as "Obstruction"?

"Obstruction" in K80 codes refers to:

  • Cystic duct obstruction - stone blocking the cystic duct
  • Common bile duct obstruction - stone blocking the CBD
  • Clinical signs: jaundice, elevated bilirubin, dilated bile duct on imaging

Evidence There is NO Obstruction:

  • ✅ Normal liver function tests (bilirubin 12 μmol/L - normal)
  • ✅ Ultrasound: CBD measures 4mm (normal, not dilated)
  • ✅ Intraoperative cholangiogram: "Good flow of contrast into duodenum. No filling defects. No stones visualized in biliary tree."
  • ✅ No jaundice documented

When Would You Use K80.11?

Examples requiring ".11" (WITH obstruction):

  • Stone visualized in common bile duct on imaging/cholangiogram
  • Dilated CBD (>7mm) on ultrasound
  • Elevated bilirubin/ALP suggesting biliary obstruction
  • Documentation states "obstructive jaundice" or "biliary obstruction"
💡 Pro Tip: If the cholangiogram shows "no stones in bile duct" and "good flow into duodenum," there is NO obstruction → use ".10"

When to Code Intraoperative Cholangiogram:

From Course Slides:
"For surgical day care visits, ERCP/ERC [and intraoperative cholangiogram] is always captured."

What is an Intraoperative Cholangiogram?

It's an X-ray of the bile ducts taken DURING surgery (while gallbladder is being removed) to:

  • Check for stones in the common bile duct
  • Confirm normal anatomy of bile ducts
  • Ensure no injury to bile ducts during dissection

CCI Code Breakdown:

3.OE.10.WX
├─ 3 = Diagnostic imaging
├─ OE = Bile ducts (anatomical site)
├─ 10 = Radiography (X-ray)
└─ WX = Operative/intraoperative approach

Other Cholangiogram Codes:

Procedure CCI Code
Intraoperative cholangiogram 3.OE.10.WX
Endoscopic retrograde cholangiogram (ERC) 3.OE.10.WZ
Intravenous cholangiogram 3.OE.10.XA
💡 Remember: "Operative" approach (WX) means it's done DURING surgery. "Endoscopic" (WZ) means via scope. "Intravenous" (XA) means dye injected into vein.

Approach Coding for Cholecystectomy:

1.OD.89.DA = Laparoscopic cholecystectomy
1.OD.89.LA = Open cholecystectomy (laparotomy)

CCI Code Breakdown:

1.OD.89.DA
├─ 1 = Therapeutic intervention
├─ OD = Gallbladder (anatomical site)
├─ 89 = Excision, total (complete removal)
└─ DA = Laparoscopic (endoscopic) approach

Evidence of Laparoscopic Approach:

  • ✅ "Four trocars inserted" (ports for laparoscopic instruments)
  • ✅ "10mm umbilical port (camera)"
  • ✅ "Laparoscopy revealed..."
  • ✅ "Gallbladder removed through umbilical port"
  • ✅ NO mention of laparotomy incision

What If Converted to Open?

From Course Slides:

"When [laparoscopic procedure] is converted to open approach, the procedure is coded as an 'Open' approach with the Status attribute of 'C' assigned to capture the conversion."

Example: Started laparoscopic but converted to open → Code: 1.OD.89.LA with Status = C (converted)

💬 Test Your Understanding

💡 Interview Scenarios: Practice making coding decisions based on different clinical scenarios!

Question 1: Acute vs Chronic

Same patient, same procedure, BUT pathology report says: "Acute cholecystitis with neutrophilic infiltration and abscess formation." What changes?

✅ Answer:

Main Diagnosis changes to K80.00 (Calculus of gallbladder with acute cholecystitis, without obstruction)

All interventions stay the same:
- 1.OD.89.DA (laparoscopic cholecystectomy)
- 3.OE.10.WX (intraoperative cholangiogram)

Rationale: Pathology report is the definitive source for acute vs chronic. If pathology says "acute," code as K80.0x regardless of clinical presentation.

Question 2: With Obstruction

What if the cholangiogram showed: "Stone visualized in distal common bile duct, 8mm. Dilated CBD measuring 9mm. No flow into duodenum." What changes?

✅ Answer:

Main Diagnosis changes to K80.11 (Calculus of gallbladder with chronic cholecystitis, WITH obstruction)

Additional code needed: K80.50 (Calculus of bile duct without cholangitis or cholecystitis, without obstruction) - because there's a SEPARATE stone in the bile duct

Possible additional intervention: If the CBD stone was extracted during the procedure, add 1.OE.57.^^ (Extraction, bile ducts)

Rationale: Stone in bile duct causing obstruction = ".11" code. The CBD stone is coded separately from the gallbladder stones.

Question 3: Converted to Open

What if the surgeon started laparoscopically but encountered severe adhesions and converted to open laparotomy to complete the cholecystectomy safely?

✅ Answer:

Main Diagnosis stays K80.10 (chronic cholecystitis without obstruction)

Cholecystectomy code changes to: 1.OD.89.LA (open approach) with Status attribute = "C" (converted from laparoscopic)

Cholangiogram stays: 3.OE.10.WX (if performed before conversion)

Rationale: Per course slides: "When [laparoscopic] is converted to open approach, code as 'Open' with Status 'C' to capture the conversion." The final approach determines the code.

Question 4: T-Tube Drainage

What if the surgeon placed a T-tube in the common bile duct after removing a stone, and left it in place for drainage? What additional code is needed?

✅ Answer:

Add intervention code: 1.OE.52.DA-TS (Drainage, bile ducts, laparoscopic approach, using T-tube)

From course slides: "A T-tube may be inserted into the bile duct when the surgeon suspects stones or other obstruction... Add additional code for bile duct drainage 1.OE.52.^^"

Code Breakdown:
- 1.OE = Bile ducts
- 52 = Drainage
- DA = Laparoscopic approach
- TS = Using T-tube device
