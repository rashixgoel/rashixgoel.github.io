Case Study: Preterm Infant with Respiratory Distress Syndrome

📊 Complexity: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ Expert 🏥 Setting: NICU 📅 LOS: 14 days

📋 Case Overview

Patient: Male newborn
Gestational Age: 34 weeks 5 days
Birth Weight: 2,180 grams
Delivery: Cesarean section

🎯 Key Learning Objectives:

  • Most responsible diagnosis selection for unhealthy newborns
  • Z38 coding - Type M for healthy, Type 0 for unhealthy newborns
  • Prematurity: Code BOTH gestational age (P07.37) AND birth weight (P07.14)
  • RDS (P22.0) vs TTN (P22.1) distinction
  • Preterm-associated jaundice coding (P59.0)
  • Rule-out sepsis coding (Z03.71)
  • CPAP and phototherapy intervention coding

NEWBORN DISCHARGE SUMMARY

INFANT: Male

MOTHER: 32 years old, G3P2→3

DATE OF BIRTH: February 10, 2026, 03:47

DATE OF DISCHARGE: February 24, 2026

LENGTH OF STAY: 14 days

BIRTH HISTORY:

  • Gestational Age: 34 weeks 5 days (PRETERM)
  • Birth Weight: 2,180 grams (LOW BIRTH WEIGHT)
  • Delivery: Cesarean section (repeat, planned)
  • Apgar Scores: 7 at 1 minute, 8 at 5 minutes

HOSPITAL COURSE:

Day of Life 1:

Infant born via planned repeat C-section. Emerged with mild respiratory distress—grunting, nasal flaring, intercostal retractions. Admitted to NICU.

Chest X-ray: Bilateral ground-glass appearance with air bronchograms—consistent with Infant Respiratory Distress Syndrome (RDS).

Treatment: Nasal CPAP started at +6 cm H2O, IV fluids (NPO initially), Antibiotics started (ampicillin + gentamicin) pending cultures.

Day of Life 2:

Respiratory status improved. Blood culture at 48 hours: NO GROWTH → Antibiotics discontinued. Started trophic feeds.

Day of Life 3:

Weaned off CPAP to room air. Breathing well.

NEW ISSUE: Infant appeared jaundiced. Bilirubin: 242 μmol/L. Blood types: Mother A+, Infant O+. Diagnosis: Neonatal jaundice (physiologic + prematurity). Phototherapy started.

Days 4-5:

Phototherapy continued. Bilirubin decreased to 172 μmol/L. Phototherapy stopped Day 5.

Day 6:

Rebound bilirubin stable at 165 μmol/L.

Days 7-14:

Feeding advancement, weight gain established. Transitioned to full breastfeeding.

DISCHARGE DIAGNOSES:

  1. Preterm male infant, 34 5/7 weeks, delivered by cesarean
  2. Infant respiratory distress syndrome (resolved)
  3. Neonatal jaundice requiring phototherapy (resolved)
  4. Rule-out sepsis (culture negative)

DISCHARGE WEIGHT: 2,320 grams

DISCHARGE CONDITION: Stable

DISPOSITION: Home with mother

🧠 Coding Rationale & Decision Points

💡 Being able to explain your coding decisions is just as important as getting the codes right!

The Rule for Unhealthy Newborns:

This baby is UNHEALTHY because they have clinically significant conditions (RDS, prematurity, jaundice) that:
  • Required NICU admission
  • Required therapeutic interventions (CPAP, phototherapy)
  • Extended length of stay to 14 days

From Fletcher & Lokun, Page 30.3:

"When a newborn is considered unhealthy (i.e. has a clinically significant condition affecting management and/or length of stay), the appropriate code from Chapter XVI or Chapter XVII is assigned as the most responsible diagnosis."

"A code from Z38.- is mandatory on all newborn records. When any other code is assigned as the most responsible diagnosis, a code from Z38.- is assigned as a diagnosis type 0."

Why P22.0 (RDS) is Type M:

  • RDS required CPAP (major respiratory intervention)
  • RDS drove the NICU admission
  • RDS was the acute condition requiring immediate treatment
  • Without RDS, this baby might have been managed differently
❌ Common Error: Using Z38.01 as Type M for all newborns
✅ Correct: Z38 is Type M ONLY for healthy newborns. For unhealthy newborns, Z38 is Type 0.

Prematurity Requires TWO Codes:

P07.37 - Gestational age (34 weeks)
Shows organ maturity level, affects clinical management
P07.14 - Birth weight (2,180g = low birth weight)
Shows growth status, affects thermal regulation and feeding

Why Both?

They're independent risk factors:

  • Some babies are preterm but appropriate weight for gestational age
  • Some are term but low weight (IUGR/SGA)
  • This baby is BOTH preterm AND low birth weight
  • Each condition independently impacts care and monitoring
💡 How to Count Weeks:
34 weeks + 5 days = 34 COMPLETED weeks → P07.37 (the "7" in P07.37 means 34 weeks)

This Case = P22.0 (Respiratory Distress Syndrome)

Evidence for RDS:
  • ✓ Preterm infant (34 weeks - classic risk factor)
  • ✓ Immediate onset at birth
  • ✓ CXR: "ground-glass appearance, air bronchograms" (pathognomonic for RDS)
  • ✓ Required CPAP support
  • ✓ Gradual improvement over 3 days

Key Distinction:

Condition Key Features
P22.0 - RDS Preterm, ground-glass CXR, surfactant deficiency, needs respiratory support
P22.1 - TTN Term or near-term, "wet lung" on CXR, retained fetal lung fluid, resolves quickly (24-48 hrs) with minimal support

Understanding Type 1 (Comorbid) Diagnoses:

From Fletcher & Lokun, Page 30.3:

"Additional codes to identify other clinically significant conditions present at birth are assigned diagnosis type 1."

Why Each Condition is Type 1:

P07.37 (Prematurity):
  • Required extended NICU monitoring
  • Risk factor for multiple complications
  • Impacts discharge timing
P59.0 (Jaundice):
  • Required phototherapy (therapeutic intervention)
  • Required serial bilirubin monitoring
  • Extended length of stay
P07.14 (Low birth weight):
  • Required feeding support and monitoring
  • Independent risk factor from prematurity
  • Affects thermal regulation
Z03.71 (Rule-out sepsis):
  • Empiric antibiotics administered
  • Blood cultures drawn
  • Influenced initial management
💡 Key Principle: If a condition required treatment, monitoring, or extended the length of stay, it's Type 1. There is NO Type 2 or Type 3 on newborn abstracts!

💬 Test Your Understanding

Question 1: Gestational Age Change

What if this baby was born at 32 weeks instead of 34 weeks?

✅ Answer:

Change P07.37 to P07.35 (32 completed weeks instead of 34 weeks)

Everything else stays the same. The code changes because gestational age is different, but the clinical scenario (RDS, jaundice, etc.) would be similar or possibly more severe.

Type M would still be P22.0 (RDS remains the most responsible diagnosis)

Question 2: Positive Blood Cultures

What if blood cultures came back positive for E. coli instead of negative?

✅ Answer:

Remove Z03.71 (rule-out sepsis - no longer a "rule-out")

Add P36.4 (Sepsis of newborn due to E. coli) as Type 1

Consider if Type M changes: If sepsis became the dominant clinical issue requiring the longest treatment, P36.4 might become Type M instead of P22.0. Clinical judgment based on which condition drove length of stay and resource use.

Question 3: Term Infant with RDS

What if baby was 39 weeks (term) but still had RDS?

✅ Answer:
  • Remove P07.37 and P07.14 (no prematurity/low birth weight)
  • Keep P22.0 as Type M (RDS can rarely occur in term infants, e.g., from maternal diabetes or C-section without labor)
  • Change P59.0 to P59.9 (not preterm-associated jaundice, would be unspecified neonatal jaundice)
  • Z38.01 stays the same (cesarean delivery) or changes to Z38.00 if delivered vaginally

Question 4: Healthy Baby Scenario

What if this same baby (34+5 weeks, 2,180g) had NO respiratory distress, NO jaundice, and went home on Day 2?

✅ Answer:

Z38.01 becomes Type M (baby is now considered HEALTHY)

P07.37 and P07.14 become Type 0 (or not coded at all)

Rationale: Per Fletcher & Lokun page 30.3, "When a healthy, liveborn infant is born in hospital or is admitted immediately after birth, having not previously been admitted to any other hospital, a code from Z38.- is assigned as the most responsible diagnosis. A newborn is considered healthy when factors such as prematurity, small birth hematoma, or slight jaundice are considered to have no effect on the health of the infant."

If the preterm infant had no complications and was discharged quickly, they would be considered "healthy" despite technical prematurity.
